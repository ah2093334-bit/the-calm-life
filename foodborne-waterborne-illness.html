Foodborne and Waterborne Illness: What Actually Makes You Sick

⚠️ This article is for general education only and is not medical advice. Severe vomiting, diarrhea, or dehydration should be evaluated by a doctor.

Most stomach illness people casually call food poisoning is caused by bacteria, viruses, or parasites contaminating food or water, usually from unsafe handling rather than the food itself being unsafe.

How contamination actually happens

Harmful microbes typically enter food or water through unwashed hands, cross-contamination between raw and cooked food, inadequate cooking temperatures, or unsafe water sources.

Common culprits

Bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli are frequently linked to undercooked meat, unwashed produce, or unpasteurized dairy. Viruses such as norovirus spread easily through contaminated water or surfaces. Parasites in contaminated water are a major cause of illness in areas with unsafe water supplies.

Why symptoms vary so much

Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and stomach cramps are common across most foodborne illnesses, but timing varies. Some cause symptoms within hours, others take days.

Reduces risk

  • Washing hands before eating or cooking
  • Cooking meat to safe temperatures
  • Washing produce thoroughly
  • Drinking treated or bottled water when unsure
  • Refrigerating leftovers promptly

Increases risk

  • Leaving food at room temperature too long
  • Cross-contaminating raw and cooked food
  • Drinking untreated water from unclear sources
  • Undercooking meat or eggs
  • Ignoring expiration dates on perishables

When it's more than a stomach bug

High fever, blood in stool, signs of dehydration, or symptoms lasting more than a couple of days are reasons to see a doctor rather than wait it out.
