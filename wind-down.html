Sleep

How to Actually Wind Down at Night

"Just put your phone away" is the most common sleep advice — and the least useful on its own. Your body needs more than one signal to know it's time to slow down. Here's what actually helps.

1. Dim the lights an hour before bed

Bright light tells your brain it's still daytime, which delays the release of melatonin, the hormone that makes you feel sleepy. Switching to lamps instead of overhead lights in the evening makes a real difference.

2. Do one calming activity, not zero activities

Lying in bed trying to "do nothing" often makes the mind more active, not less. Reading a few pages, stretching, or journaling gives your brain something gentle to focus on instead.

3. Keep your room slightly cool

Your body temperature naturally drops as you fall asleep. A cooler room supports that process, while a warm room fights against it.

4. Set a consistent "shutdown" time

Going to bed at a wildly different time each night confuses your body's internal clock. Even a rough, consistent window helps your body learn when to expect rest.

5. Write down tomorrow's first task

A lot of nighttime restlessness comes from your brain trying to hold onto things so it doesn't forget them. Writing down just one thing — your first task tomorrow — gives your mind permission to let go for the night.

None of this requires new gadgets or apps. It's about giving your body clear, consistent signals that the day is done.
